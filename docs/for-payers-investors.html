For payers + investors

What healthcare loses when the gap between visits stays opaque.

Between clinical visits, the gaps are where patterns vanish. A patient’s medication adherence drifts. A dietary barrier emerges that the care plan didn’t anticipate. A sign of clinical change that would normally surface at the next appointment — now six months out — instead surfaces in the emergency department, or not at all.

For programs built on food-as-medicine, this gap is expensive. Preventable utilization rises. Intervention timing narrows. The data that would prove the program’s value — the connection between what the care team designed and what the patient actually experienced — fragments across systems.

Cena is being built to close it.

The value Cena is being built to provide.

What we’re building surfaces the insights that today get caught only in hindsight.

  • Between-visit intelligence. The care team sees adherence patterns, emerging barriers, and clinical changes as they happen — not months later. Actionable, real-time.
  • Preventative intervention. Patterns that would otherwise trigger an ED visit or an urgent visit get surfaced to the care team first — so the program can intervene before the system does.
  • Reduced ED and hospital utilization. When food-as-medicine programs run on better between-visit data, patient outcomes improve and system burden drops.
  • Automatic value-based reporting. Cena is being built to produce the outcome documentation your VBC contracts require — capturing intervention → adherence → outcome → re-assessment — automatically from the program’s workflow, not from spreadsheets.

The substrate is designed so the care team’s existing program keeps running on its existing rhythm. What changes is what the care team sees, and what the program can demonstrate.

Cena is being built to run underneath what you’ve built, not instead of it.

If your program is one of these, you know the gap.

You’ve already built something. You’ve invested in the program’s structure, trained the staff, proven the model works for your patients. The fragmentation isn’t failure — it’s the cost of running food-as-medicine on infrastructure built for something else.

That’s the gap Cena is being built to consolidate.

When Cena is live, your program keeps running on the clinical workflows you’ve designed. Your dietitians keep their assessment protocols. Your case managers keep their outreach cadence. Your program’s IRB-approved intervention stays exactly what you built. What changes is that the clinical actions your team takes — assessments, care plans, adherence check-ins, outcome measurements — carry a unified trail. One system sees what your program is doing. One report reflects what your program achieved.

What stays yours: clinical authority (your team retains every decision); data ownership (your program owns the patient relationship); branding and external messaging (your program is the protagonist when you talk about it). Cena is the layer underneath that captures the data your team needs to prove what you’re solving.
