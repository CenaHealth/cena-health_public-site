## At a glance

- **Purpose:** Template (contract) for every future `/for-[contact-or-org-slug]` instance. When a specific named contact (e.g. *"Dave"* at a partner health system) or partner-org (e.g. *"UConn Health,"* *"Hartford HealthCare"*) lands on the page — usually via a personal note from Aaron or Vanessa — they land on a page that frames Cena around *their* specific work, *their* program, *their* stated priorities. Per IA §13 + voice.md §7.
- **Primary audience:** A specific named contact or partner-org. Authored per-instance with custom context — Cena's read of how the substrate fits into *their* specific processes. Per voice.md §7 + IA §13.
- **Headline pattern (template, locked):** *"Cena, for [contact-name-or-org]."* No commas; no honorific; no *"Dear."* Per voice.md §7.
- **Subhead pattern (template, locked):** *"How the substrate maps to [their specific named program / initiative / focus]."* Per voice.md §7.
- **Voice register:** Researched, alignment-named, never presumptuous. Voice should read as *"we paid attention to your specific work before reaching out."* Inherits the comms-peer-doored translated/useful/named-contact-shaped register from `/for-comms` per voice.md §2 + §7. NOT flattery; NOT vendor-portal pitch; NOT generic "for partners" page.
- **CTA architecture:** *"Direct line to [Aaron / Vanessa / whoever owns this conversation]."* Calendly or direct contact, never form-gated. Per voice.md §9 named-contact CTA + §7 per-contact template voice.
- **HIPAA-adjacent:** **Template itself: No.** Per-instance basis: typically no (per-contact landings frame substrate-mapping, not data-handling specifics). Any instance that surfaces HIPAA / PHI / BAA claims routes through HDG handoff per citation spec §8 before publish.
- **Launch-gating partner-input dependencies (template-level):**
  - **Item #1 (UConn-named upgrade)** — touches any instance that references the live pilot for substantiation (e.g., a `/for-uconn-health` instance literally cannot ship without the named clearance; other instances that mirror pilot-reference language inherit the generic-AMC default until clearance lands).
  - **Other items (#2, #3, #4)** — do not touch the template directly; would touch any instance that surfaces architecture / data-residency / chain-of-custody claims, which routes through the same Cena-architecture citation discipline as `/how-the-substrate-works`.
- **Instance-shipping gate** (load-bearing for the template): no per-contact instance ships until (a) Cena has operational backing to maintain it as an authoritative artifact (the contact's specific context doesn't go stale silently), (b) brand-fidelity at instance-authoring time clears the credibility bar (typography / spacing / asset placement matches the rest of the site automatically — hand-authored per-contact landings that LOOK like one-offs become credibility liabilities), and (c) the named contact / org warrants the investment (per voice.md §7 + IA §13 honest limit).

### Section 1: Personalized hero (H1 + subhead)

**Heading pattern (template-locked):** *"Cena, for [contact-name-or-org]."* H1, verbatim pattern; only the bracketed token fills per instance.

**Subhead pattern (template-locked):** *"How the substrate maps to [their specific named program / initiative / focus]."* Subhead pattern; the bracketed token fills per instance with the contact's specific named work — *"the HIV-nutrition program you've been building,"* *"UConn Health's food-as-medicine initiative,"* *"Hartford HealthCare's diabetes-management pilot,"* etc.

**Position:** Section 1, canonical per IA §13 + voice.md §7.

**Length / shape:** H1 (pattern-locked) + 1-sentence subhead (pattern-locked). ≤40 words combined. No hero CTA at instance level; the named-contact CTA lives at page-foot per voice.md §9 + §7.

**What this section's contract requires:**
- Render the H1 pattern verbatim per voice.md §7 — the bracketed token is the only edit per instance. The phrase *"Cena, for [X]"* does voice work simultaneously: (1) "for" frames the page as authored-for-this-contact, not as a generic capability presentation; (2) the comma + name combination reads as conversational without being presumptuous (*"Dear Dave"* would be presumptuous; *"For Dave"* without the comma reads as a marketing segment).
- Subhead front-loads what *their* work is (the bracketed token), not what Cena does. The verb *"maps to"* is the load-bearing voice mechanic — Cena's substrate is a thing that *maps to* the contact's program; the contact's program is the protagonist that the mapping is *to*. Reversing this (e.g., *"How [their program] benefits from Cena's substrate"*) collapses partner-protagonist into vendor-flattery.
- Maintain the *"we paid attention before reaching out"* register from voice.md §7. The H1 + subhead together must read as *"this page was authored for this conversation, not adapted from a template"* — even though it IS adapted from a template, the per-contact fill in the subhead's bracketed token does the personalization work.
- Hold the structural posture: Cena is the **substrate** being mapped; the **contact / partner-org's specific named program** is the protagonist. This frame carries through every subsequent section.

**What this section's contract forbids:**
- Filling the H1 bracketed token with anything other than a real contact name or partner-org name. No category labels (*"For Cardiology"*), no role titles (*"For CMOs"*), no descriptors (*"For Innovators"*). Those collapse into segment-marketing and break the per-contact framing — they would belong on a different IA surface.
- Filling the subhead bracketed token with generic capability language (*"how the substrate works,"* *"how our platform integrates"*). The subhead's job is naming the contact's *specific* named program / initiative — generic capability language signals the page wasn't actually authored for this contact.
- Adding a hero CTA button — the named-contact CTA lives at page-foot per voice.md §9.
- Using "media kit" / "press kit" / "marketing collateral" anywhere in the hero — voice.md §4 forbidden.
- Foregrounding Cena as the actor — sentences like *"Cena's substrate brings X to your program"* collapse the partner-protagonist frame.
- Quoting outcome percentages or dollar figures in the hero (voice.md §5 + citation spec §4 stale-claim ban).
- Naming the contact with an honorific (*"Dr. Dave"* / *"Mr. Smith"*) — voice.md §7 explicit *"no honorific."* The contact's preferred reference shape is established in the contact's public work; mirror it.

**Required claims:**
- None as factual claims requiring citation at template level. The H1 pattern is positioning; the subhead's bracketed token (the contact's specific named program) is research-grounded at instance time and cites the public source (see Section 2 citation discipline for the inherited citation rules).
- Implicitly claims that this page was authored specifically for the named contact. The implicit claim is substantiated by Section 2 (the research-grounded observation paragraph) — never claimed in the hero itself.

**Required proof / artifacts:**
- None inline at template level. Per-instance: the subhead's bracketed-token research-grounding is substantiated by Section 2's citations.

**Voice cues (template):**
- The hero's job at instance time is signaling *"this page was authored for you, not adapted from a template you've seen on five other healthcare-tech sites."* The H1 pattern is fixed; the personalization happens in the precision of the subhead's bracketed token. *"How the substrate maps to UConn Health's HIV-nutrition program"* lands; *"How the substrate maps to your healthcare initiatives"* fails.
- Test against voice.md §7 exemplars: *"We've been following [contact]'s work on [specific topic]"* lands; *"We deeply admire [contact]'s incredible leadership"* does not. The hero subhead inherits the same posture — researched, specific, never flattering.
- Test against voice.md §1 quiet competence: the hero earns the reader's *"these people did their homework"* through what's specific in the subhead, not through what's elevated in the H1.

**Forbidden phrasings (this section, template-level):**
- "Welcome [name]!" / "Hello [name]!" / "Dear [name]" — voice.md §7 explicit no honorific + collapses register into form-letter shape.
- "Cena and [partner-org]: a partnership" — claims relationship that may not exist; voice.md §7 must-not-say *"Never claim a relationship that does not exist."*
- "Built for healthcare innovators like you" — segment-marketing collapse; voice.md §4 + §7.
- "Transform [your program] with Cena" — voice.md §4 forbidden superlative + collapses partner-protagonist.
- "Our partnership with [partner-org]" / "Working together with [contact-org]" — relationship-claim; voice.md §7 must-not-say *"Never claim a relationship that does not exist ('our partner,' 'our customer' for someone who isn't)."*

**Lens-specific notes:**
- BV: H1 + subhead pattern is locked per voice.md §7. The structural elements — pattern-locked H1, research-grounded subhead — are what make every instance feel authored even though the contract is templated. If a future instance feels generic, the failure is almost always in the subhead's bracketed-token fill (too vague, not specific to *their* named work).
- HDG (template): no HIPAA-adjacent copy at hero level. Per-instance: if a contact's named work is itself a HIPAA-regulated program (e.g., a hospital's HIV-nutrition program), the subhead's bracketed token names *the program* (which is public knowledge) without claiming about the *program's data* (which is not).

### Section 2: What we observed about your work (research-grounded context paragraph)

**Heading pattern (template-locked):** Quiet H2. Pattern: *"What we observed about your work"* or close voice-safe variant (*"What we've been reading,"* *"Where your work shows up"*). Front-load research-grounded posture per voice.md §3 inheritance rule. Anchor `#what-we-observed` (per-instance).

**Position:** Section 2, directly under hero. This ordering is deliberate per voice.md §7: voice should read as *"we paid attention to your specific work before reaching out"* — and that posture is established by leading with observation, not by leading with what Cena does. The contact reads "you noticed *me*" before they read "and here's how we'd fit."

**Length / shape:** 1 paragraph (~150-200 words per instance) naming Cena's read of the contact's specific work. Each claim about the contact's work cites a public source inline per citation discipline §2 row "Partner-specific" (or Cena-research-with-public-source equivalent — see citation discipline below).

**What this section's contract requires:**
- Per-instance fill: research-grounded summary of the contact's / org's public work. Sources are public-only — the contact's LinkedIn, their organization's website, their public talks / publications, their organization's stated priorities documents (annual reports, press releases, board statements).
- Each substantive claim about the contact's work carries an inline citation marker (`[^N]`) resolving to a References section at page-foot per citation spec §5.
- Voice rule: paraphrase, never quote without explicit permission. Per voice.md §7. A direct quote requires partner-attested confirmation; without it, paraphrase + cite the source where the position was expressed.
- The paragraph names *what* the contact has been doing publicly, not *who* the contact is as a person. Focus on the work, not the personality. (*"You've been publishing on food-as-medicine outcomes since 2022; your recent talk at AcademyHealth named the gap between RCT evidence and partner-program implementation as the field's open question"* — lands. *"You're a thoughtful leader passionate about healthcare innovation"* — fails.)
- Surfaces 2-3 specific public-source-grounded observations, not a long list. Specificity > coverage.
- Per voice.md §7 must-not-say guardrails:
  - Never invent a quote from the contact.
  - Never claim a relationship that does not exist (*"our partner,"* *"our customer"* for someone who isn't).
  - Never include outcomes data the contact would not have agreed to publish (e.g., un-attributed pilot outcomes).

**What this section's contract forbids:**
- Speculating about the contact's internal state, private statements, or confidential program data. The page is built only from what the contact (or their organization) has *publicly* said or published. Per voice.md §7 + IA §13 honest limit. This is the single most important guardrail in the template.
- Using flattery or admiration language (*"deeply admire,"* *"incredible leadership,"* *"transformative work in the field"*) — voice.md §7 explicit *"The fill is researched, not flattering."*
- Citing the contact's work without a public source — every claim about their work must be public-source-citable. If the only source is *"Vanessa mentioned in passing,"* that's not a public source and the claim doesn't ship.
- Naming Cena's substrate features in this section — Section 3 is where the substrate-mapping lands; Section 2's job is establishing what *they're* doing.
- Implying outcomes for the contact's program that the contact hasn't publicly claimed (*"Your program achieved X reduction in readmissions"* — unless the contact has publicly published that figure with citation).
- Naming PHI or partner-confidential program data anywhere — even if Cena somehow knows it. The template forbids it because per-contact landings must be defensible-on-publish to the contact themselves (they should read the page and see only what they've already said publicly).

**Required claims (template-level discipline; per-instance fills):**
- Every substantive claim about the contact's / org's work carries a public-source citation per citation discipline §2 row "Partner-specific" (extended to include "Cena-research-with-public-source" sub-category):
  - **Cena-research-with-public-source citation type:** LinkedIn profile / public talk / published article / organization's website / publicly released report. Cited with retrieval-date stamp + re-check cadence per citation spec §5.
  - **Re-check cadence:** `on-partner-comms-shift` (default) — the cited public source may move, be edited, or be removed. The view-block treatment in citation spec §6 applies particularly well to per-contact landings because the public sources cited are the ones most likely to drift.
- Direct quotes (if any): partner-attested per citation spec §2(c). Without partner attestation, paraphrase only.

**Required proof / artifacts:**
- Inline citation markers (`[^N]`) on every substantive claim about the contact's / org's work, resolving to References section at page-foot.
- No screenshots, embedded talks, or asset displays inline — the citations carry the substantiation; the section's job is showing Cena's read, not re-publishing the contact's content.

**Voice cues (template):**
- The section's load-bearing voice mechanic is *specificity over flattery*. A reader who finishes the paragraph should think *"they actually read my [talk / paper / annual report]"* — never *"they wrote nice things about me."*
- Test against voice.md §7 fill voice: *"We've been following [contact]'s work on [specific topic]"* lands; *"We deeply admire [contact]'s incredible leadership"* does not. The Section 2 paragraph extends *"following the work on the specific topic"* into 2-3 specific observations.
- Test against voice.md §3 specificity-over-generality: each observation names a concrete primitive of the contact's work (a specific talk; a specific publication; a specific program element) — never a generic descriptor (*"your innovative work in healthcare"*).
- Per voice.md §7: *"The contact is the protagonist of their own work; Cena is the substrate that might map to it."* Section 2 holds the contact as protagonist; Section 3 introduces the substrate as mapping-candidate.

**Forbidden phrasings (this section, template-level):**
- "We deeply admire / are inspired by / have long respected [contact]'s work" — voice.md §7.
- "Your visionary leadership" / "your transformative approach" / "your incredible work" — voice.md §4 superlatives + voice.md §7 flattery ban.
- "We know you / We've heard great things about you" — voice.md §7 must-not-say (claims relationship that doesn't exist).
- "[Contact] said X" without partner-attestation — invented quote; voice.md §7 explicit ban.
- "Your patients" / "your program's outcomes" — unless publicly published by contact; speculation about internal state.
- "Based on our research" / "From what we've gathered" — meta-commentary on Cena's research process; collapses the "we paid attention" register into self-narration.

**Lens-specific notes:**
- BV: Specificity is the load-bearing mechanic. Voice fails when observations could apply to any healthcare leader; voice succeeds when each observation cites a specific named primitive of the contact's work.
- HDG (template): no HIPAA-adjacent claims at Section 2 level. Per-instance check: if a public source the section cites itself contains regulatory-sensitive language about the contact's program, Cena's paraphrase routes through citation discipline §8 HDG handoff before publish.
- CTA (when lens lands): every citation in this section must resolve to a real public source that says what Cena says it says. Per-instance citation review is non-negotiable per citation spec §7 pre-ship gate.

### Section 3: How Cena fits — named alignments to their stated priorities

**Heading pattern (template-locked):** Quiet H2. Pattern: *"How Cena fits"* or close voice-safe variant (*"Where the substrate maps,"* *"How this connects to [contact's named work]"*). Front-load mapping-posture per voice.md §3. Anchor `#how-cena-fits` (per-instance).

**Position:** Section 3, after the research-grounded observation paragraph. Section 2 established the contact as protagonist of their own work; Section 3 introduces Cena's substrate as a candidate that *maps to* their work — never the other way around.

**Length / shape:** 2-3 named alignments per instance, in parallel card / panel structure. Each alignment: short heading naming the specific alignment (1 line) + paragraph (~80-120 words) describing how Cena's substrate maps to that specific element of the contact's work + inline deep-link to the relevant Cena surface (`/for-clinical-leaders`, `/how-the-substrate-works/provenance-and-accountability`, `/your-program-on-cena`, `/reference-program`, etc.). Per voice.md §7: *"2-3 specific places Cena's substrate maps to their stated priorities. Each named alignment includes a deep-link into the relevant Cena surface."*

**What this section's contract requires:**
- Each named alignment cites a specific element of the contact's work (referenced from Section 2's observations) and names how Cena's substrate maps to it. Per-instance fills are research-grounded; the *shape* of the alignment is templated.
- Example structural shape (template, instance-fills bracketed):
  - **Alignment 1:** *"Your work on [specific named program element from Section 2] maps to Cena's [specific substrate capability]."* + 80-120 word paragraph naming the specific mapping mechanics + deep-link to the relevant Cena surface.
- Alignments are *substantive*, not aspirational. Per voice.md §7 must-not-say: *"Never name a Cena substrate capability that isn't real today (no aspirational present-tense)."* If a contact's named work would map to a capability Cena doesn't yet ship, that alignment is named in Section 4 (Honest gap) — not Section 3.
- Each alignment deep-links to the Cena surface where the substrate capability is substantiated. The deep-link is the *"don't take our word for it — see the substantiation"* mechanism. Per-instance: the deep-link target depends on which substrate capability the alignment names (provenance-and-accountability if the alignment is audit-trail-shaped; for-clinical-leaders if clinical-authority-shaped; etc.).
- Maintain the substrate-mapping frame: Cena's substrate *maps to* the contact's program; never *brings,* *delivers,* or *augments.* Per voice.md §1 substrate-not-vendor principle.
- 2-3 alignments — not 5+, not 1. Per voice.md §7. Too few reads as thin; too many reads as scattershot.

**What this section's contract forbids:**
- Listing Cena's substrate capabilities first, then mapping to the contact's work backwards. Per voice.md §7: *"The contact is the protagonist of their own work; Cena is the substrate that might map to it."* The mapping starts from *their* work; Cena enters as the candidate.
- Aspirational mapping — naming a Cena capability that isn't real today. Per voice.md §7 must-not-say + voice.md §5 site-wide forbidden ("aspirational copy in present tense").
- Mapping to outcomes data the contact hasn't publicly published (*"Your program's 30% readmission reduction maps to Cena's audit-grade outcomes capture"* — unless the contact has publicly published that figure with citation).
- Generic capability descriptions (*"Cena's powerful AI platform supports your innovative work"*) — voice.md §4 forbidden + collapses the specific-mapping discipline into vendor-marketing.
- Listing more than 3 alignments — voice.md §7 explicit *"2-3 specific places."*
- Mapping to internal-only Cena vocabulary (*"capability matrix,"* *"provenance trail,"* *"reference-program blueprint"*) — voice.md §4 internal-vocab forbidden + collapses the comms-translated register.
- Naming the vendor stack (Anthropic, Google Cloud, Vertex AI) in any alignment — HDG packet row 3.

**Required claims (template-level discipline; per-instance fills):**
- Each named substrate capability cited in an alignment requires citation per citation spec §2 row "Cena-specific architecture":
  - **Citation source:** Vault canon docs in `Knowledge/Areas/Meta/Entities/cena-health/` with dated commit reference; or engineering attestation from named Cena team member with date.
  - **Re-check cadence:** `on-architecture-change`.
- Each cited element of the contact's work (from Section 2) carries the citation already attached in Section 2; Section 3 references the citation rather than re-citing.
- Deep-links to Cena surfaces: each linked surface must itself have substantiation for the substrate capability the alignment names (i.e., a `/for-clinical-leaders` deep-link only works if the linked page substantiates the clinical capability the alignment claims).

**Required proof / artifacts:**
- 2-3 inline deep-links to Cena substrate surfaces (`/for-clinical-leaders`, `/how-the-substrate-works/provenance-and-accountability`, `/your-program-on-cena`, `/reference-program`, `/how-the-substrate-works/integration`, etc.) — per-instance choice based on which capabilities the alignments name.
- Citation markers (`[^N]`) on Cena-substrate-capability claims, resolving to References section.

**Voice cues (template):**
- The section's load-bearing voice mechanic is *named-and-substantiated mapping*. A reader who finishes the section should think *"they named where their stuff connects to mine, and they showed me where to verify"* — never *"they pitched at me with capability bullets."*
- Per voice.md §7 fill voice: alignments are *researched, not flattering* + protagonist-respecting. The contact's work is the load-bearing input; Cena's substrate is the candidate.
- Each alignment passes the *"could a competitor steal this and have it work as well?"* test per voice.md §3. If a competitor's substrate could be mapped to the same contact-work element with the same alignment language, the alignment is too generic — rewrite to name a specifically Cena-shaped capability.

**Forbidden phrasings (this section, template-level):**
- "Cena's powerful / industry-leading / next-generation / AI-powered platform" — voice.md §4 superlatives + AI-marketing forbidden.
- "Transform your program with [capability]" — voice.md §4 + collapses partner-protagonist.
- "Empower your team to [outcome]" — voice.md §4 vendor-flattery + uncited outcome claim.
- "Our partnership with [partner-org]" — voice.md §7 relationship-claim ban.
- "Cena delivers / brings / provides [capability] to [your program]" — substrate-not-vendor inversion; use *"maps to,"* *"connects to,"* *"runs underneath."*
- "[Capability] that's perfect for your needs" — generic + flattery + uncited.
- "Cena's AI" / "AI platform" / "intelligent automation" — voice.md §4 + §5.
- "Solutions" / "seamless" / "comprehensive" — voice.md §4 forbidden.

**Lens-specific notes:**
- BV: The specific-mapping voice is the load-bearing mechanic; alignments must read as researched cross-references between *their* work and *Cena's* substrate, with both ends substantiated. Generic capability claims collapse the page even if the rest of the contract holds.
- CTA (when lens lands): each substrate capability cited in an alignment must match current shipped fidelity per citation spec §2 + outstanding-partner-input.md items #2-4. If an alignment names a capability that depends on an outstanding partner-input item (e.g., end-to-end US-only data residency per item #2), the alignment either (a) waits until the item resolves, or (b) ships with the hedged-down phrasing per HDG packet recommendations.
- HDG (template + per-instance): any alignment that names HIPAA / PHI / BAA / encryption / access-control claims routes through HDG handoff per citation spec §8 before publish. The template's default is *"BAA-ready architecture"* (HDG row 2 approved) rather than *"HIPAA-compliant"* (HDG row 3.1 forbidden); per-instance alignments inherit.

### Section 4: What we'd want to talk about (specific topics / questions for the conversation)

**Heading pattern (template-locked):** Quiet H2. Pattern: *"What we'd want to talk about"* or close voice-safe variant (*"Questions we'd bring to a conversation,"* *"Where we'd want your read"*). Front-load conversation-shaped posture per voice.md §3. Anchor `#what-wed-want-to-talk-about` (per-instance).

**Position:** Section 4, after the substrate-mapping alignments. The ordering is deliberate: by Section 4, the contact has seen (1) the page was authored for them, (2) Cena read their public work, (3) the substrate maps to specific named elements of their work. Section 4 gives them an agenda *before* the conversation — preparing them for what the named-contact CTA in Section 5 would actually discuss.

**Length / shape:** 2-3 specific topics or questions in parallel structure. Each topic: 1-line topic-heading + 2-3 sentences naming why Cena wants their read on it. ~150-250 words total.

**What this section's contract requires:**
- Per-instance fill: 2-3 specific topics or questions Cena would actually bring to a conversation with this contact. Each topic is grounded in the contact's public work (referencing Section 2) or in the mapping alignments (Section 3) — never generic.
- Topics are *honest questions*, not pitch-disguised-as-questions. Per voice.md §7 fill voice: *"The fill is researched, not flattering."* A topic shaped as *"How could Cena partner with [contact] on [generic shape]?"* fails — it's a pitch in question form. A topic shaped as *"You named the gap between RCT evidence and partner-program implementation as the field's open question; we'd want to hear how that maps to your [specific named program]'s phase-2 decisions"* lands.
- The **Honest gap section is encouraged here**, per voice.md §7: *"Honest gap section (optional but encouraged): one paragraph naming where Cena's substrate does not yet fit their work, and what would have to change for it to. Reads as competence; the contact's vetters will look for this section."* The honest gap can be one of the 2-3 topics if it earns its place.
- Each topic is *short enough to be a real agenda item*, not a presentation. The page is preparing the contact for a 30-60 minute conversation, not for a vendor evaluation.
- The section signals that Cena treats the conversation as *substantive*, not as a sales touchpoint. This posture is the section's load-bearing voice mechanic.

**What this section's contract forbids:**
- Pitch-disguised-as-questions (*"How would [partner-org] like to partner with Cena on this?"* — that's a pitch).
- Topics that frame the conversation as Cena selling and the contact buying. Per voice.md §1 substrate-not-vendor + voice.md §7 protagonist-respecting.
- Topics that are too long to be agenda items (>3 sentences per topic).
